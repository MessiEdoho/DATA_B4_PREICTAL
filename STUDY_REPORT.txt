1. DATA ACQUISITION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Continuous single-channel electroencephalographic (EEG) recordings were
obtained from 102 mice enrolled in the UNIQURE preclinical epilepsy
study. Each recording was acquired as a single EEG channel digitised at
a sampling frequency of 500 Hz and stored in European Data Format (EDF).
One EDF file was generated per subject (e.g., m1.edf). Recording
durations varied across subjects, ranging from several hours to multiple
days of uninterrupted monitoring.

Seizure events were annotated independently in Excel spreadsheets (.xlsx),
with one annotation file per subject (e.g., m1_xlsx.xlsx). Each row
specified the onset ('start_time') and offset ('end_time') of a single
electrographic seizure in absolute datetime format (DD/MM/YYYY
HH:MM:SS.fff). For computational use, all annotation timestamps were
converted to elapsed seconds relative to the EDF recording start time
extracted from the file header.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. DATA PARTITIONING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

To ensure unbiased evaluation, the 102 subjects were assigned to three
mutually exclusive partitions at the animal level prior to any model
development:

    Partition       Subjects    Purpose
    --------------- ----------- -------------------------------------------
    Training        72          Model fitting and hyperparameter tuning
    Validation      10          Early stopping, threshold optimisation
    Test            20          Final held-out evaluation (reserved)

Subject-level partitioning guarantees that no EEG data from the same
animal appears in more than one partition. This design prevents inflated
performance estimates that arise when temporally correlated segments from
a single recording are distributed across training and evaluation sets
(Gemein et al., 2020; Roy et al., 2019) [VERIFY EXACT QUOTE BEFORE
SUBMISSION].

2.1 Justification of the 72/10/20 partition

    The 102-mouse cohort was divided to balance three competing
    requirements: (i) maximising the training signal available to the
    deep temporal models under evaluation, (ii) reserving a validation
    partition large enough for stable early-stopping and decision-
    threshold selection but small enough not to erode the final
    test-set allocation, and (iii) securing a held-out test partition
    large enough that subject-level generalisation can be reported
    with meaningful uncertainty bounds.

    Allocating 72 subjects (approximately 71%) to training reflects
    the known sensitivity of convolutional and recurrent temporal
    models to the diversity and volume of available EEG, where
    representational coverage of both seizure morphologies and
    background activity improves appreciably with additional
    recordings (Roy et al., 2019) [VERIFY EXACT QUOTE BEFORE
    SUBMISSION]. Under the present segmentation scheme, 72 training
    subjects contribute 65,510 ictal segments (Section 8.1), an order
    of magnitude large enough to support supervised learning of the
    minority class without relying on loss-level reweighting
    (Sections 9.2-9.3).

    The 10-subject validation partition (approximately 10%) is sized
    to yield a sufficient count of ictal segments (11,603 under the
    present segmentation) for stable macro-F1 estimation during
    per-epoch model selection, while keeping its footprint small
    enough for a single tuning study to evaluate dozens of
    hyperparameter configurations within the cluster's walltime
    budget (Section 7.1.1). A smaller validation partition would
    inflate trial-to-trial selection variance during Optuna search;
    a larger one would displace data from the held-out test
    partition without meaningfully stabilising the tuning signal.

    The 20-subject test partition (approximately 20%) is deliberately
    set larger than validation. Because the test partition is queried
    only once per final model and is reported alongside per-subject
    metric variability (Section 7.6), its size directly governs the
    width of the confidence intervals associated with the reported
    generalisation estimate. Twenty held-out subjects provide a
    reasonable cross-section of the UNIQURE cohort's biological
    heterogeneity (strain background, age, seizure burden, artefact
    profile) and permit both cohort-level summary statistics and
    per-subject breakdowns without any single animal dominating the
    aggregate estimate.

    An automated leakage check is executed at the time of partition
    generation to confirm that no subject identifier appears in more
    than one split. This check is enforced programmatically each time
    the data manifest is regenerated.

References:
    Gemein, L. A. W., Schirrmeister, R. T., Chrabąszcz, P., Wilson, D.,
        Boedecker, J., Schulze-Bonhage, A., Hutter, F., & Ball, T. (2020).
        Machine-Learning-Based Diagnostics of EEG Pathology. NeuroImage,
        220, 117021. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuroimage.2020.117021
    Roy, Y., Banville, H., Albuquerque, I., Gramfort, A., Falk, T. H.,
        & Faubert, J. (2019). Deep learning-based electroencephalography
        analysis: a systematic review. Journal of Neural Engineering,
        16(5), 051001.

3. PREPROCESSING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Prior to segmentation, each continuous EEG recording was subjected to
a three-stage preprocessing pipeline applied sequentially:

(i) Powerline interference removal. A second-order zero-phase IIR notch
    filter centred at 50 Hz (quality factor Q = 30) was applied using
    forward-backward filtering (scipy.signal.filtfilt) to suppress mains
    interference while preserving the amplitude and phase of adjacent
    frequency components.

(ii) Baseline correction. The arithmetic mean of each processing block
     was subtracted to eliminate any DC offset introduced by the recording
     hardware or electrode drift.

(iii) Robust amplitude normalisation. Each block was normalised using
      the robust z-score:

          z = (x - median(x)) / (1.4826 * MAD(x))

      where MAD denotes the median absolute deviation. The scaling
      constant 1.4826 ensures consistency with the standard deviation
      under a Gaussian distribution (Rousseeuw & Croux, 1993). The
      median and MAD were chosen over the mean and standard deviation
      because of their resistance to the large-amplitude transients
      characteristic of rodent EEG, including grooming artefacts and
      movement-related spikes.

To manage memory constraints on the high-performance computing cluster,
the preprocessing pipeline was applied in contiguous blocks of 40
segments rather than loading entire recordings into memory.

Reference:
    Rousseeuw, P. J. & Croux, C. (1993). Alternatives to the Median
        Absolute Deviation. Journal of the American Statistical
        Association, 88(424), 1273-1283.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. SEGMENTATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Each preprocessed recording was divided into fixed-length segments using
a sliding window approach with the following parameters:

    Parameter           Value       Description
    ------------------- ----------- -----------------------------------------
    Window length       2,500       5 seconds at 500 Hz
    Step size           1,250       2.5 seconds (50% overlap)
    Segment dimensions  (1, 2500)   Single channel, 2,500 time points

A 50% overlap between consecutive windows was employed to augment the
effective training set size and to preserve temporal continuity across
adjacent segments. Each segment was stored as an individual NumPy array
file (.npy) in 32-bit floating-point format, occupying approximately
10 KB per segment.

The naming convention for output files follows the pattern:
    {subject_id}_{ictal|nonictal}_{index:05d}.npy

where the index is a sequential one-based counter per class per subject
(e.g., m1_ictal_00001.npy, m1_nonictal_00003.npy). This counter
reflects the temporal order of segments within the recording but does
not encode the absolute grid position.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. SEGMENT LABELLING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Binary labels were assigned to each segment based on temporal overlap
with the annotated seizure intervals. A segment spanning the time
interval [t_start, t_end) was labelled as ictal (y = 1) if it satisfied
the overlap condition:

    t_start < seizure_end  AND  t_end > seizure_start

for at least one annotated seizure interval. Under this criterion, any
degree of temporal overlap -- including a single sample -- classifies
the segment as ictal. All remaining segments were labelled as non-ictal
(y = 0).

This any-overlap labelling strategy was adopted to maximise sensitivity
to seizure boundaries, ensuring that onset and offset segments are
included in the ictal class even when only partially overlapping with
the annotated event.

6. MODEL ARCHITECTURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Four models are evaluated in an ablation study to isolate the
contributions of multi-scale temporal processing and temporal attention:

    Model   Architecture                        Ablation role
    ------- ----------------------------------- ---------------------------
    M1      TCN                                 Baseline
    M2      TCN + Temporal Attention            + attention over M1
    M3      Multi-Scale TCN                     + multi-scale over M1
    M4      Multi-Scale TCN + Temporal Attention + both over M1

All four models share the same CausalConvBlock as their fundamental
building unit.

6.1 CausalConvBlock (residual block)

    Each residual block comprises two dilated causal one-dimensional
    convolutional layers operating at the same dilation factor
    (d = 2^L, where L denotes the layer index), following the temporal
    convolutional network (TCN) architecture proposed by Bai et al.
    (2018). The use of two convolutional layers at each dilation level
    enables multiple non-linear transformations to be learned before
    progressing to larger receptive fields, thereby enhancing
    representational capacity.

    Each convolution is followed by layer normalisation applied across
    the feature-map channel dimension, Gaussian Error Linear Unit (GELU)
    activation, and spatial dropout (Dropout1d). Layer normalisation is
    employed in place of batch normalisation to mitigate the effects of
    inter-subject variability in EEG amplitude and the instability of
    batch statistics under relatively small batch sizes (16-64). Spatial
    dropout (nn.Dropout1d) is used instead of element-wise dropout
    (nn.Dropout) to provide structured regularisation appropriate for
    convolutional feature maps. For a tensor of shape
    (batch, num_filters, time), spatial dropout zeros entire
    feature-map channels across the full time dimension, whereas
    element-wise dropout zeros individual (feature-map, time-step)
    cells independently. Because adjacent time steps within a single
    convolutional feature map are highly correlated (they share
    overlapping receptive fields through the same filter), element-
    wise dropout on a single cell is trivially recovered from its
    temporal neighbours and produces weak regularisation. Spatial
    dropout removes an entire feature map for a given forward pass,
    forcing representational redundancy across the num_filters
    channels (Tompson et al., 2015). Note that "channel" in this
    context refers to a learned convolutional feature map, NOT to an
    EEG recording electrode: the input EEG is single-channel (one
    electrode per recording), so the input tensor has shape
    (batch, 1, time); the num_filters dimension is introduced after
    the first convolution. GELU activation is adopted to facilitate
    smoother gradient propagation and improved modelling of
    continuous biosignal dynamics. A 1x1 pointwise convolution is used to form the residual
    skip connection when required for dimensionality matching. Causal
    padding with right-side trimming is applied throughout to preserve
    temporal causality and prevent leakage of future information during
    sequence modelling.

    Architecture diagram (single CausalConvBlock):

    Input x (F_in channels, T time steps)
    |
    |--[ Residual path ]--------------------------------------------.
    |                                                               |
    +--[ Conv1d(F_in, F_out, k, dilation=d, causal padding) ]      |
    |       |                                                       |
    |       +--[ Trim right (remove future samples) ]               |
    |       |                                                       |
    |       +--[ LayerNorm (channel dim) ]                          |
    |       |                                                       |
    |       +--[ GELU ]                                             |
    |       |                                                       |
    |       +--[ Dropout1d (spatial, feature-map-channel-wise) ]    |
    |                                                               |
    +--[ Conv1d(F_out, F_out, k, dilation=d, causal padding) ]     |
    |       |                                                       |
    |       +--[ Trim right ]                                       |
    |       |                                                       |
    |       +--[ LayerNorm ]                                        |
    |       |                                                       |
    |       +--[ GELU ]                                             |
    |       |                                                       |
    |       +--[ Dropout1d ]                                        |
    |                                                               |
    +--[ + ]<------[ 1x1 Conv (if F_in != F_out) ]<----------------'
    |           or identity (if F_in == F_out)
    |
    Output (F_out channels, T time steps)

    The receptive field of L stacked CausalConvBlocks with exponentially
    increasing dilation (d = 1, 2, 4, ..., 2^(L-1)) and two
    convolutions per block is:

        RF = 2 * (2^L - 1) * (k - 1) + 1

    where k is the kernel size. For example, with L = 6 layers and
    k = 7, the receptive field spans 757 samples (1.514 s at 500 Hz).

6.2 Shared output components

    While the four models differ in their temporal feature extraction
    strategy (single-branch vs multi-scale, uniform vs attention-based
    aggregation), they share a common downstream pathway from temporal
    aggregation through to the training objective. This standardised
    output design ensures that performance differences across the
    ablation study are attributable to the upstream architectural
    variation rather than to differences in the classification head,
    loss function, optimiser, or training procedure.

    (i) Temporal aggregation: global average pooling.

        After the convolutional backbone processes a 5-second EEG
        segment, the resulting temporal feature map H has shape
        (batch, F, T), where F denotes the number of convolutional
        filters and T denotes the number of output time steps. Because
        causal padding preserves the input length, T remains equal to
        the original 2,500 samples throughout the convolutional stack.

        This high-dimensional sequence must be reduced to a fixed-
        length vector before classification. In M1 (TCN) and M3
        (Multi-Scale TCN), this reduction is performed by global
        average pooling, which computes the arithmetic mean of the
        feature vectors across all T time steps:

            z = (1/T) * sum_{t=1}^{T} h_t        z in R^F

        This operation collapses the temporal dimension entirely,
        producing a single F-dimensional summary vector z that
        represents the average activation pattern over the full
        5-second segment. By treating every time step with equal
        weight, global average pooling assumes that seizure-
        discriminative information is distributed uniformly across
        the segment rather than concentrated at specific temporal
        locations.

        In M2 (TCN + Attention) and M4 (Multi-Scale TCN + Attention),
        global average pooling is replaced by a learned temporal
        attention mechanism (described in Section 6.4). Rather than
        assigning uniform weight 1/T to every time step, the
        attention module learns data-dependent weights alpha_t that
        allow the model to focus on the most seizure-informative
        portions of the segment. This replacement is the central
        architectural variable in the M1-vs-M2 and M3-vs-M4 ablation
        comparisons.

    (ii) Classification head: single linear layer.

         The aggregated feature vector z in R^F -- whether obtained
         from global average pooling (M1, M3) or from the attention-
         weighted context vector (M2, M4) -- is projected to a single
         scalar output by a fully connected linear layer:

             logit = w^T z + b

         where w in R^F is the learned weight vector and b in R is
         the bias term. This layer contains F + 1 trainable parameters
         and produces an unbounded real-valued output (the logit) that
         represents the model's unnormalised confidence that the input
         segment contains seizure activity.

         Critically, no activation function is applied after this
         layer during training. The raw logit is passed directly to
         the loss function, which internally applies the sigmoid
         transformation. This design avoids the numerical instability
         that arises from computing log(sigmoid(x)) separately, as
         discussed below.

         At inference time, the sigmoid function is applied explicitly
         to convert the logit to a probability estimate p in [0, 1]:

             p = sigma(logit) = 1 / (1 + exp(-logit))

         A segment is classified as ictal if p >= threshold, where
         the threshold is either the default value of 0.5 or the
         Youden-optimal threshold determined from the validation set.

    (iii) Training objective: binary cross-entropy with logits.

          The network is trained to minimise the binary cross-entropy
          (BCE) loss, computed using PyTorch's BCEWithLogitsLoss. This
          function fuses the sigmoid activation and the BCE computation
          into a single operation that exploits the log-sum-exp
          identity for numerical stability:

              L(logit, y) = -[ y * log(sigma(logit))
                            + (1 - y) * log(1 - sigma(logit)) ]

          where y in {0, 1} is the ground-truth binary label (1 for
          ictal, 0 for non-ictal) and sigma denotes the sigmoid
          function. When the logit is a large positive number and y=1,
          or a large negative number and y=0, the naive computation of
          log(sigma(.)) would suffer from floating-point underflow.
          The fused implementation avoids this by reformulating the
          expression using numerically stable primitives.

          The pos_weight parameter, which scales the loss contribution
          of positive (ictal) samples, is set to 1.0. Class imbalance
          is instead addressed entirely through offline stratified
          downsampling of the training corpus (Section 9). This
          separation of concerns ensures that the loss function treats
          each training sample with equal weight, preventing the
          gradient magnitude distortion that can arise when pos_weight
          is set to large values (e.g., 438 for the raw 1:438
          imbalance ratio). Such distortion would disproportionately
          amplify gradients from ictal samples, potentially causing
          optimiser instability and overconfident predictions on the
          minority class.

6.3 M1: TCN (baseline)

    L CausalConvBlocks are stacked sequentially with exponentially
    increasing dilation factors (d = 1, 2, 4, ..., 2^(L-1)). The
    output is globally average-pooled along the temporal axis and
    passed through a linear classification head producing a single
    logit for binary seizure detection.

    Input (1 x 2500)
    |
    +--[ CausalConvBlock, d=1   ]
    +--[ CausalConvBlock, d=2   ]
    +--[ CausalConvBlock, d=4   ]
    +--[ ...                    ]
    +--[ CausalConvBlock, d=2^(L-1) ]
    |
    +--[ Global Average Pool (T -> 1) ]
    |
    +--[ Linear(F, 1) ]
    |
    logit

    Tunable hyperparameters: num_layers (L), num_filters (F),
    kernel_size (k), dropout rate.

6.4 M2: TCN + Temporal Attention

    M2 replaces the global average pooling of M1 with a two-layer
    additive temporal attention mechanism that learns to assign
    non-uniform importance weights to individual time steps. The
    TCN backbone is identical to M1; the only architectural
    difference is the aggregation stage.

    6.4.1 Motivation

    Global average pooling treats every time step equally, which
    implicitly assumes that seizure-discriminative information is
    distributed uniformly across the 5-second segment. In practice,
    seizure onset or offset patterns may be concentrated in a
    fraction of the window, while the remainder contains background
    EEG that dilutes the discriminative signal. A learned attention
    mechanism allows the model to focus on the most informative
    temporal regions, potentially improving both classification
    accuracy and interpretability (the attention weights form a
    temporal saliency map over the raw EEG segment).

    6.4.2 Two-layer additive attention: step by step

    The TCN backbone produces a temporal feature sequence:

        H = [h_1, h_2, ..., h_T]       H in R^(T x F)

    where T = 2,500 (the segment length preserved by causal padding)
    and F = num_filters (the channel dimension from the final
    CausalConvBlock). Each h_t in R^F is a learned representation of
    the EEG signal at time step t.

    The attention mechanism processes H in four stages:

    Stage 1 -- Projection (Linear + tanh):

        e_t = tanh(W_a * h_t + b_a)        for each t = 1, ..., T

        W_a in R^(D_a x F) is a learnable weight matrix that projects
        each F-dimensional feature vector h_t into a D_a-dimensional
        attention space. b_a in R^(D_a) is a learnable bias. The
        hyperbolic tangent (tanh) activation squashes each element of
        the projected vector to the range [-1, +1], introducing the
        non-linearity that allows the scoring function to capture
        feature interactions that a purely linear projection cannot.

        D_a (attention_dim) is a tunable hyperparameter controlling
        the dimensionality of this intermediate representation. A
        larger D_a increases the expressiveness of the scoring function
        but adds parameters. In this study, D_a is selected from
        {32, 64, 128} by Optuna.

    Stage 2 -- Scoring (Linear, no bias):

        s_t = v^T * e_t                     for each t = 1, ..., T

        v in R^(D_a) is a learnable vector (implemented as
        nn.Linear(D_a, 1, bias=False)) that reduces each D_a-
        dimensional energy vector e_t to a single scalar score s_t.
        This score represents the unnormalised importance of time
        step t. The absence of bias ensures that the scoring function
        is centred: a zero-energy vector produces a zero score.

    Stage 3 -- Normalisation (Softmax):

        alpha_t = exp(s_t) / sum_{j=1}^{T} exp(s_j)

        The softmax function converts the T unnormalised scores into
        a proper probability distribution over time steps. The
        resulting attention weights alpha_t satisfy two properties:
        (a) alpha_t >= 0 for all t (non-negativity), and (b)
        sum_{t=1}^{T} alpha_t = 1 (normalisation). A time step with
        a high score receives a large weight; a time step with a low
        score receives a near-zero weight.

    Stage 4 -- Aggregation (Weighted sum):

        c = sum_{t=1}^{T} alpha_t * h_t        c in R^F

        The context vector c is a weighted average of the original
        feature vectors h_t, where the weights alpha_t are determined
        by the learned scoring function rather than fixed at 1/T.
        Unlike global average pooling, this allows the model to
        concentrate its representation on the temporal regions most
        relevant to seizure detection.

    After aggregation, dropout is applied to the context vector c
    (with rate attention_dropout, tunable in [0.0, 0.4]) to
    regularise the attention output and prevent the model from
    relying on a single dominant time step. The regularised context
    vector is then passed to the shared linear classification head
    (Section 6.2).

    6.4.3 Learnable parameters

    The attention module introduces three sets of learnable parameters:

        Component           Shape               Parameters
        ------------------- ------------------- --------------------
        W_a (projection)    (D_a x F)           D_a * F + D_a (bias)
        v   (scorer)        (D_a x 1)           D_a (no bias)
        Dropout             --                  0 (no learnable params)

        Total attention parameters: D_a * (F + 2)

    For D_a = 64 and F = 64: 64 * 66 = 4,224 parameters. This is
    a small fraction (~2-5%) of the total model parameters, meaning
    the attention mechanism adds minimal computational overhead while
    providing temporal selectivity and interpretability.

    6.4.4 Interpretability: attention as saliency

    The attention weights alpha_t provide an intrinsic interpretability
    mechanism. By inspecting alpha_t across the T = 2,500 time steps,
    one can visualise which temporal regions the model considers most
    relevant for its seizure/non-seizure decision. A method
    get_attention_weights(x) is provided that extracts these weights
    for any input segment, enabling:

      - Per-segment saliency maps overlaid on raw EEG waveforms
      - Class-averaged attention profiles (mean alpha_t for ictal vs
        non-ictal segments)
      - Differential attention analysis (ictal minus non-ictal mean
        attention, highlighting time steps where the model attends
        more to seizure segments)

    These visualisations are produced automatically during training
    (Figure 13 in the training scripts).

    6.4.5 Architecture diagram

    Input (1 x 2500)
    |
    +--[ TCN backbone: L CausalConvBlocks, d=1,2,...,2^(L-1) ]
    |
    |  H: (batch, F, T) -- temporal feature maps
    |
    +--[ Transpose -> (batch, T, F) ]
    |
    |  For each time step t:
    |
    +--[ Linear(F, D_a) ]----> W_a * h_t + b_a     (projection)
    |       |
    |       +--[ tanh ]------> e_t in R^(D_a)       (non-linear energy)
    |       |
    |       +--[ Linear(D_a, 1, no bias) ]--> s_t   (scalar score)
    |
    +--[ Softmax over T ]----> alpha_t               (attention weights)
    |                          alpha_t >= 0, sum = 1
    |
    +--[ Weighted sum: c = sum(alpha_t * h_t) ]      (context vector)
    |                          c in R^F
    |
    +--[ Dropout(attention_dropout) ]                (regularisation)
    |
    +--[ Linear(F, 1) ]                              (classification head)
    |
    logit

    6.4.6 Design choices and justification

    The two-layer additive formulation (project + tanh + score) was
    chosen over simpler alternatives for two reasons:

    (a) A single-layer linear scorer (s_t = w^T h_t) computes a
        fixed linear combination of feature channels. It cannot learn
        non-linear feature interactions (e.g., "attend to time steps
        where channel A is high AND channel B is low"). The
        intermediate tanh projection enables such interactions.

    (b) Multi-head self-attention (Transformer-style) computes
        pairwise interactions between all T^2 time step pairs. With
        T = 2,500, this would produce a 2,500 x 2,500 attention
        matrix per head, adding substantial memory and compute cost
        for a binary classification task where the output is a single
        scalar, not a sequence. The additive formulation computes
        T independent scores with O(T) complexity.

    This formulation is identical in M2 (over the single-branch TCN
    backbone) and M4 (over the multi-scale TCN backbone), ensuring
    that the M2-vs-M4 comparison isolates the backbone architecture
    rather than attention design differences.

    Additional tunable hyperparameters: attention_dim (D_a),
    attention_dropout. (Full search space in Section 7.3.)

    Ablation: M2 vs M1 isolates the contribution of learned temporal
    attention over uniform average pooling. If M2 outperforms M1,
    the temporal attention mechanism captures seizure-relevant
    temporal structure that uniform averaging fails to exploit.

6.5 M3: Multi-Scale TCN

    Three parallel branches process the input simultaneously, each
    operating at a different temporal resolution defined by its
    dilation schedule:

        Branch 1 (fine):         dilations [1,  2,   4]   -- spike morphology
        Branch 2 (intermediate): dilations [8,  16,  32]  -- rhythmic bursts
        Branch 3 (coarse):       dilations [32, 64, 128]  -- seizure evolution

    Each branch consists of three CausalConvBlocks arranged
    sequentially -- one block per dilation value. For example,
    Branch 1 contains: block at d=1, followed by block at d=2,
    followed by block at d=4. Since each CausalConvBlock internally
    comprises two dilated convolutional layers (as described in
    Section 6.1), each branch contains 3 blocks x 2 convolutions
    = 6 convolutional layers, and the full three-branch model
    contains 18 convolutional layers in parallel.

    Importantly, each branch is NOT a single residual block. The
    dilation list [1, 2, 4] specifies three separate blocks stacked
    in sequence within that branch, with each block operating at a
    progressively larger dilation factor. This stacking within each
    branch provides a branch-local receptive field given by:

        RF_branch = 1 + 2 * (k - 1) * sum(dilations)

    At the maximum tuned kernel size k=7:
        Branch 1:   RF =  85 samples =  170 ms at 500 Hz
        Branch 2:   RF = 673 samples = 1.35 s
        Branch 3:   RF = 2689 samples = 5.38 s (spans full 5-s segment)

    The dilation schedules are selected so the three branches jointly
    cover the physiological temporal scales of rodent EEG seizures:
    sub-second spike complexes, ~0.4-1.4 s rhythmic bursts, and
    full-segment ictal envelopes. The coarse branch's 5.38 s
    receptive field guarantees that each segment is classified with
    full temporal context available at fusion.

    All three branches use a geometric doubling progression
    (d_{i+1} = 2 * d_i). This follows the canonical TCN dilation
    pattern established by Bai et al. (2018), under which each
    added block approximately doubles the branch receptive field.
    The progression is deliberately regular across all three
    branches: the fine branch begins the series at d=1, and the
    intermediate and coarse branches continue the same doubling
    pattern starting from higher offsets (d=8 and d=32). This
    uniformity is adopted for architectural and reporting clarity
    rather than as a training-stability claim -- a single, regular
    doubling rule applied across branches is simpler to describe,
    reproduce, and compare against other TCN variants in the
    literature than ad-hoc per-branch schedules would be.

    Branch outputs are fused (concatenation with 1x1 projection, or
    element-wise average), globally average-pooled, and classified.

    Input (1 x 2500)
    |
    +----------------+------------------+------------------+
    |                |                  |                  |
    | Branch 1       | Branch 2         | Branch 3         |
    | Block d=1      | Block d=8        | Block d=32       |

7. HYPERPARAMETER OPTIMISATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Hyperparameters were optimised using the Tree-structured Parzen
Estimator (TPE) algorithm implemented in Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019).
A two-stage tuning strategy was employed: backbone architectural and
training hyperparameters were tuned first, and attention-specific
hyperparameters were tuned subsequently with the backbone weights
frozen. This sequential design ensures that the M1-vs-M2 and M3-vs-M4
ablation comparisons share identical backbone configurations.

7.1 Common tuning protocol

    For each model, 50 Optuna trials were conducted using the TPE
    sampler with 10 initial random exploration trials, after which
    Bayesian kernel density estimation guided subsequent sampling
    (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012). Each trial trained a candidate model
    for a maximum of 20 epochs, with early stopping triggered when
    the validation macro F1-score failed to improve for 5 consecutive
    epochs. Within each trial, the AdamW optimiser (Loshchilov &
    Hutter, 2019) was employed with cosine annealing learning rate
    decay, in which the learning rate was reduced from the
    trial-specific initial value to 1% of that value over the
    20-epoch budget. Gradient clipping with a maximum norm of 1.0
    was applied at each parameter update step to stabilise training
    in deep temporal convolutional architectures.

    To reduce computational cost, a MedianPruner was configured to
    terminate underperforming trials after 3 warm-up epochs: once a
    trial's intermediate validation F1 fell below the running median
    of all completed trials, training was halted and the trial was
    marked as pruned. The pruner was inactive during the first 10
    trials to allow a sufficient baseline of completed evaluations
    for robust median estimation.

    A fixed random seed of 42 was set at the start of every trial
    to ensure reproducible weight initialisation across all
    hyperparameter configurations. During tuning, a stratified 1%
    subset of the validation partition was used for per-epoch
    evaluation, reducing validation cost while preserving the
    original class ratio (see Section 7.1.1 for the timing
    justification and sample-size analysis).

    Table 2. Common hyperparameter tuning configuration.

    Parameter               Value
    ----------------------- -------------------------------------------

    Max epochs per trial    20
    Early stopping patience 5 epochs
    Random startup trials   10
    Pruner                  MedianPruner (n_warmup_steps = 3)
    Sampler                 TPESampler (seed = 42)
    Optimiser               AdamW
    LR scheduler            CosineAnnealingLR (eta_min = lr x 0.01)
    Loss function           BCEWithLogitsLoss (pos_weight = 1.0)

    Validation subset       1% stratified (see Section 7.1.1)
    Mixed precision         AMP (FP16 on CUDA, FP32 fallback)

    The 1% subset preserves 116 ictal segments (= 11,603 x 0.01), which
    is sufficient for stable F1-score ranking during hyperparameter
    optimisation. With 116 positive cases, the standard error on
    recall at an operating point of p = 0.9 is approximately 2.8%
    (sqrt(p(1-p)/n)), which is substantially smaller than the F1 gap
    typically observed between good and poor hyperparameter
    configurations. This precision is consistent with established
    practice in medical imaging and EEG literature, where
    hyperparameter selection and early stopping decisions are
    routinely based on validation sets containing fewer than 150
    positive cases (Rajpurkar et al., 2017; Roy et al., 2019).

    The 1% subset is drawn once per tuning script with a fixed seed
    (42) and is held constant across all 50 trials within that
    script, ensuring that trial-to-trial F1 differences reflect
    genuine hyperparameter effects rather than subset sampling
    variability. The full validation partition is reserved for
    (a) early-stopping and checkpoint selection during final model
    training (Section 7.6), and (b) threshold-selection analyses
    reported alongside the held-out test set. The test partition is
    never accessed during tuning.

    The four architectural hyperparameters (num_layers, kernel_size,
    num_filters, dropout) jointly determine the model's capacity to
    represent temporal patterns in the EEG signal, while the three
    training hyperparameters (learning_rate, weight_decay, batch_size)
    control how effectively the optimiser navigates the resulting loss
    landscape. All seven are tuned jointly because architectural and
    training choices interact: for example, deeper networks with more
    filters may require stronger regularisation (higher dropout or
    weight decay) to avoid overfitting, while wider networks may
    tolerate larger batch sizes without gradient noise becoming
    problematic.

    Architectural constraint: the receptive field (RF) of a TCN
    determines the number of consecutive input samples that can
    influence each output time step. For the detection of seizure
    events that unfold over at least one second of EEG, the RF
    must span a minimum of 500 samples at 500 Hz. The RF of L
    stacked CausalConvBlocks, each containing two dilated
    convolutions with exponentially increasing dilation factors
    (d = 1, 2, ..., 2^(L-1)), is given by:

        RF = 2 * (2^L - 1) * (k - 1) + 1

    where k is the kernel size and the factor of 2 accounts for
    the two convolutions per block. Hyperparameter configurations
    for which this expression evaluates to fewer than 500 samples
    are rejected before training begins (the trial is pruned
    immediately), avoiding the computational cost of a full
    20-epoch evaluation on an architecturally inadequate model.
    With num_layers restricted to [6, 9], only the single (L=6,
    k=3) combination produces RF < 500 (RF = 253 samples, 0.51 s);
    the other 11 of 12 (L, k) combinations pass, giving a 92%
    acceptance rate at the sampling stage. For example, L = 6
    with k = 7 yields RF = 757 samples (1.51 s) and is accepted.

7.3 M2: Temporal attention hyperparameter search space

    The TCN backbone is instantiated from M1's best hyperparameters
    (best_params.json) and its weights are frozen during attention
    tuning. Only the temporal attention module and classification
    head receive gradient updates.

    Backbone freezing serves three purposes. (Ablation integrity)First, it preserves
    the controlled ablation design: because M1 and M2 share an
    identical, independently optimised backbone, any performance
    difference between them can be attributed solely to the
    temporal attention mechanism rather than to confounded changes
    in backbone configuration. If backbone parameters were
    co-tuned with attention hyperparameters, the search would
    conflate backbone adaptation with attention contribution,
    undermining the ablation. Second (search efficiency), freezing reduces the number
    of trainable parameters from the full model (~80K-200K) to
    the attention module alone (~4K-10K), which substantially
    narrows the effective search space and allows the 50-trial
    budget to converge more efficiently on attention-specific
    hyperparameters. Third (backbone stability), freezing eliminates the risk of
    catastrophic drift in backbone representations during
    attention tuning: without frozen weights, large attention
    learning rates could destabilise the pre-optimised feature
    extractor. Note that freezing applies only during this
    hyperparameter search; during final model training (Section
    7.6), all parameters are unfrozen and trained jointly.

7.6 Final model training protocol

    Each of the four ablation models (M1-M4) is trained from scratch
    on the full training partition after hyperparameter optimisation.
    The protocol is identical in structure across all four models;
    differences arise only in (a) the hyperparameters loaded from the
    corresponding tuning-output JSON and (b) the attention module
    activation for M2 and M4. A single run produces the weights,
    operating-threshold, evaluation report, and post-processing
    configuration required for downstream analysis.

7.6.1 Hyperparameter source and protocol consistency

    Each model loads its best hyperparameters from the Optuna study
    conducted in Sections 7.2-7.5:

      M1 (TCN)            : best_params.json
      M2 (TCN + Attention): best_attention_params.json
      M3 (MS-TCN)         : best_multiscale_params.json
      M4 (MS-TCN + Attn)  : best_multiscale_attn_params.json

    The hyperparameters used during training are a strict superset of
    those tuned: every Optuna-selected value is applied verbatim. No
    post-hoc hyperparameter manipulation is performed between tuning
    and training. This ensures that the reported test-set metrics
    reflect the hyperparameters selected by the validation-F1
    objective, not a subsequent hand-tuned variant.

    For reproducibility, the training scripts additionally record in
    their output JSON: the full hyperparameter dictionary, the
    training seed (42), the PyTorch and CUDA versions, the GPU model,
    the training wall-clock duration, and the git commit hash (where
    available) of the source tree at training time.

7.6.2 Optimisation

    Parameter updates use the decoupled-weight-decay variant of Adam
    (AdamW; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019):

        theta_{t+1} = theta_t - lr * (m_hat / (sqrt(v_hat) + eps)
                                      + weight_decay * theta_t)

    Decoupled weight decay is preferred over L2 regularisation
    embedded in the gradient because the latter interacts with
    adaptive step sizes in ways that have been shown to degrade
    generalisation on deep convolutional networks. Both lr and
    weight_decay are Optuna-selected per-model.

    Learning rate follows a cosine annealing schedule
    (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) over the full 100-epoch budget:

        lr(t) = eta_min + 0.5 * (lr_initial - eta_min) *
                (1 + cos(pi * t / T_max))

    with T_max = 100 and eta_min = 0.01 * lr_initial. The schedule is
    stepped once per epoch. When early stopping terminates training
    before epoch 100, the cosine cycle is necessarily truncated; the
    best-F1 checkpoint (Section 7.6.6) captures the weights at the
    peak of the realised trajectory rather than the schedule endpoint.

7.6.3 Loss function and class imbalance treatment

    Training minimises the binary cross-entropy loss with logits
    (BCEWithLogitsLoss), computed on the raw pre-sigmoid output:

        L = -[ pos_weight * y * log(sigma(z))
               + (1 - y) * log(1 - sigma(z)) ]

    with pos_weight = 1.0. Class imbalance is addressed exclusively
    through offline non-ictal selection of the training partition
    (Section 9) rather than through loss reweighting. Two manifests
    are supported: (i) the peri-ictal manifest
    (data_splits_nonictal_sampled.json, produced by
    create_balanced_splits.py) with a 1:2.37 ictal-to-non-ictal ratio,
    and (ii) the pre-ictal manifest (data_splits_T_120_sampled.json,
    produced by create_T_120_splits.py) with an approximately 1:1
    ratio (see Sections 9.2 and 9.3). Both are balanced enough for
    standard BCE optimisation without further correction.

    Offline downsampling is preferred over online pos_weight
    upweighting for two reasons: (i) it keeps the empirical gradient
    variance well-conditioned, whereas large pos_weight values
    amplify rare-class gradients disproportionately; (ii) it makes
    hyperparameter tuning results directly comparable across models,
    since the loss surface seen by Optuna is identical to the surface
    seen at final training.

7.6.4 Mixed-precision training

    On CUDA devices, forward passes and backward passes are wrapped
    in torch.amp.autocast("cuda") with FP16 arithmetic. Gradient
    scaling is managed by torch.amp.GradScaler, which dynamically
    adjusts a loss-scale factor to prevent FP16 gradient underflow
    while preserving the numerics of parameter updates in FP32. The
    BatchNorm statistics, softmax attention weights, and loss
    accumulation remain in FP32 internally.

    On the V100 (Volta), A100 (Ampere), and L40S (Ada) GPU nodes
    available in the HPC allocation, FP16 autocast enables Tensor-
    Core utilisation for matrix-multiply and convolution primitives,
    which reduces training wall time at no accuracy cost on models
    of this scale. On CPU fallbacks the autocast context is inactive
    and all operations remain FP32, so evaluation numerics are
    unchanged between cluster runs and any local debugging runs.

7.6.5 Gradient regularisation

    Gradient norms are clipped before each optimiser step using
    torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_ with max_norm = 1.0 and L2 norm:

        if ||g||_2 > 1.0:  g <- g * (1.0 / ||g||_2)

    The clip is applied after GradScaler unscaling and before the
    optimiser step to prevent gradient explosion through the deeply
    stacked dilated convolutions. This safeguard is particularly
    relevant for M1 at high num_layers (up to 8) and M3/M4 with
    kernel_size = 7 and dilation = 128 in the coarse branch, where
    the effective depth along the coarse receptive field reaches
    6 layers of rescaling.

7.6.6 Early stopping and checkpoint selection

    Early stopping is governed by validation macro F1-score evaluated
    on the complete validation partition (4,298,154 segments), not
    on the 1% tuning subset. After each epoch:

        if val_macro_f1[t] > best_val_f1:
            best_val_f1 = val_macro_f1[t]
            best_epoch  = t
            epochs_no_imp = 0
            save weights to {model}_best_weights.pt
        else:
            epochs_no_imp += 1
            if epochs_no_imp >= 10:
                break

    The patience of 10 epochs is intentionally less aggressive than
    the patience of 5 used during tuning: during tuning the objective
    was fast comparative ranking, whereas during final training the
    goal is to give the cosine schedule sufficient time to anneal
    through any intermediate plateaux in the loss landscape.

    Two checkpoints are maintained:
      - {model}_best_weights.pt: weights at the epoch of highest
        validation macro F1. These weights are used for all reported
        test-set evaluations (Section 9).
      - {model}_latest_weights.pt: weights at the most recently
        completed epoch, overwritten each epoch, used for crash
        recovery and warm resume (not for reporting).

    All reported metrics in Section 9 are derived from the
    best-F1 weights, not the latest weights.

7.6.7 Joint training for attention models (M2, M4)

    During hyperparameter tuning of M2 and M4 (Sections 7.3, 7.5),
    the backbone was frozen to isolate attention-specific search and
    avoid confounding ablation results by re-tuning backbone weights
    under a different loss landscape. For final training, the entire
    parameter set - both backbone and attention heads - is unfrozen
    and updated jointly. This permits the attention module and
    backbone to co-adapt end-to-end, which is consistent with the
    standard training regimen of attention-augmented CNNs in the
    action-recognition and medical-imaging literature.

    The decision to freeze only during tuning, not during final
    training, is motivated by the search-efficiency / model-capacity
    trade-off: frozen tuning reduces the search space to five
    attention-specific hyperparameters (more efficient with a finite
    50-trial budget), while joint training at convergence unlocks
    the full representational capacity of the attention-augmented
    model at test time.

7.6.8 Deterministic reproducibility

    Before each training run, reproducibility is enforced by:

        random.seed(42)
        numpy.random.seed(42)
        torch.manual_seed(42)
        torch.cuda.manual_seed_all(42)

    DataLoader workers inherit worker-specific derived seeds through
    the PyTorch worker_init_fn pattern. For the cuDNN backend,
    benchmark mode is disabled and deterministic algorithms are
    enabled where supported (torch.backends.cudnn.deterministic = True,
    torch.backends.cudnn.benchmark = False). Non-deterministic atomic
    operations in certain cuDNN primitives (e.g., dilated convolutions
    with reverse-pass scatter operations) may introduce negligible
    run-to-run variability at the 1e-6 level in gradients; this has
    no measurable effect on validation F1 at the precision reported
    in Section 9.

7.6.9 Post-processing of predictions

    Raw per-segment sigmoid probabilities are transformed into
    event-level detections through three sequential stages applied
    identically to all four model variants with fixed parameter
    values. Only the decision threshold may differ between models
    (Section 7.6.10); the smoothing window, refractory period, and
    minimum duration are held constant across all comparisons, so
    inter-model differences reported in Section 9 reflect model
    architecture rather than post-processing tuning.

    Stage 1 -- Probability smoothing
    --------------------------------
    A uniform moving-average kernel of width SMOOTHING_WIN = 3
    segments is convolved with the raw sigmoid probability stream
    BEFORE thresholding:

        smoothed_probs = (1/3) * (p[t-1] + p[t] + p[t+1])
        smoothed_preds = 1 if smoothed_probs >= tau else 0

    The convolution uses mode="same" to preserve segment-to-segment
    1:1 alignment. Smoothing suppresses isolated single-segment
    probability spikes caused by transient artefacts (movement,
    electrode noise), which produce a localised peak that is
    averaged down by the low-probability neighbouring segments. A
    W = 3 window was selected as the smallest odd-length kernel
    that performs meaningful averaging while minimising temporal
    smearing of genuine short-duration seizures.

    Boundary uncertainty introduced by the smoothing stage is
    bounded by floor(W/2) * step_sec = 1 * 2.5 s = +/- 2.5 s for
    event onset and offset timing. This is clinically acceptable
    for seizure detection, where onset-time resolution to the
    nearest few seconds is sufficient for downstream clinical
    interpretation.

    Stage 2 -- Refractory-period merging
    ------------------------------------
    Pairs of detected event runs separated by gaps shorter than
    REFRACTORY_SEC = 30.0 seconds are merged into a single event.
    This corrects for transient probability dips during a single
    underlying seizure (for example, a brief period of attenuated
    ictal amplitude mid-event) that would otherwise be counted as
    two distinct alarms. The 30-second threshold is consistent
    with standard practice in automated seizure-detection
    pipelines (Shoeb & Guttag, 2010).

    Stage 3 -- Minimum duration filter
    ----------------------------------
    Events shorter than MIN_EVENT_SEC = 10.0 seconds after merging
    are discarded. This threshold is motivated by the clinical
    definition of a seizure, which typically requires sustained
    ictal activity of at least several seconds; runs shorter than
    10 s are overwhelmingly isolated false positives attributable
    to transient noise, movement artefact, or brief sub-threshold
    discharges.

    Parameter summary
    -----------------
    Parameter              Value       Rationale
    --------------------- ----------- -----------------------------
    SMOOTHING_WIN          3 segments  Suppress isolated artefact spikes
    REFRACTORY_SEC         30.0 s      Merge fragmented single-seizure runs
    MIN_EVENT_SEC          10.0 s      Discard sub-clinical events

    The three-stage pipeline converts segment-level predictions
    (of which there can be many per minute due to 50% segment
    overlap) into a clinician-facing alarm stream in which a single
    alarm corresponds to a single contiguous detected event of
    clinically plausible duration. This event-level view is the
    quantity actually experienced by a monitoring clinician and is
    the basis for the event-level false-alarm-rate metric reported
    alongside segment-level metrics in Section 9.

7.6.10 Operating-point selection (Youden's J)

    Following training, three operating thresholds are evaluated
    on the validation partition:

        Threshold 0.5 (raw, no post-processing)
        Threshold 0.5 (with post-processing from Section 7.6.9)
        Optimal threshold (with post-processing)

    The optimal threshold is selected to maximise Youden's J
    statistic (Youden, 1950):

        J(tau) = sensitivity(tau) + specificity(tau) - 1
               = TPR(tau) - FPR(tau)

    Youden's J is chosen as the selection criterion for three reasons:
    (i) it is threshold-independent in its formulation but yields a
    single-valued optimum; (ii) it weights sensitivity and specificity
    equally, which is appropriate for seizure detection where both
    missed seizures (clinical harm) and false alarms (clinician
    fatigue) are costly; (iii) it is invariant to prevalence, so the
    selected threshold generalises from the validation partition to
    the held-out test partition despite their differing ictal ratios.

    The selected threshold tau* and the underlying ROC traversal are
    saved to {model}_optimal_threshold.json and re-used verbatim at
    test time; no threshold re-selection occurs on the test set.

7.6.11 Three-tier evaluation reporting

    For each of the four models, Section 9 reports three rows of
    metrics corresponding to the progressive application of the
    decision rule:

        Row 1: raw per-segment predictions at tau = 0.5
               (isolates pure model output)
        Row 2: post-processed predictions at tau = 0.5
               (isolates post-processing contribution)
        Row 3: post-processed predictions at tau = tau*
               (final operating configuration)

    This three-tier reporting allows reviewers to attribute observed
    improvements to (a) the model architecture itself, (b) the
    morphological post-processing, or (c) the calibrated operating
    threshold, rather than conflating all three. Row 3 is the
    configuration used for all inter-model comparisons in the
    ablation analysis.

7.6.12 Compute budget and wall-clock cost

    Empirically observed on the HPC cluster (V100-PCIE-16GB and
    NVIDIA L40S partitions):

      Tuning (50 trials x ~10 epochs at 1% val subset):
        M1: ~2.5 days on L40S (~8.1 min/epoch sustained)
        M3: ~3.0 days on V100 (~10-12 min/epoch sustained)
        M2/M4 attention-only tuning: ~1.0 day each

        GPU allocations the elapsed wall time is ~2 days.

9. CLASS IMBALANCE MITIGATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

9.1 Uniform random downsampling (baseline approach)

    In the initial experimental configuration, non-ictal segments were
    downsampled to a 1:4 ictal-to-non-ictal ratio using stratified
    random sampling within each subject (random seed = 42). This
    procedure yielded a training corpus of 65,510 ictal and 262,040
    non-ictal segments (327,550 total). The binary cross-entropy loss
    function (BCEWithLogitsLoss) was configured with pos_weight = 1.0,
    as the offline downsampling served as the sole mechanism for class
    imbalance correction.

9.2 Proximity-aware downsampling (adopted approach)

    To better exploit informative training examples, a proximity-aware
    sampling strategy was subsequently adopted. Non-ictal segments were
    partitioned into two categories based on their temporal distance to
    the nearest annotated seizure boundary:

    (a) Near-seizure segments (distance <= 60 s). All non-ictal segments
        falling within a +/-60-second margin of any seizure onset or
        offset were retained in full. These peri-ictal segments
        encompass pre-ictal build-up activity, post-ictal suppression,
        and transitional EEG patterns that are morphologically proximal
        to ictal discharges (Litt & Echauz, 2002) [VERIFY EXACT QUOTE
        BEFORE SUBMISSION]. As the most challenging examples for a
        binary classifier, they contribute disproportionately to
        gradient-based learning, consistent with the principle of
        hard-example mining (Shrivastava et al., 2016) [VERIFY EXACT
        QUOTE BEFORE SUBMISSION].

    (b) Far-from-seizure segments (distance > 60 s). A random subsample
        comprising 5% of the near-seizure count was drawn from the
        remaining non-ictal pool. This preserves representational
        diversity across background EEG states (e.g., quiet sleep,
        active waking, grooming artefacts) while preventing the training
        corpus from being dominated by easily classifiable examples
        that yield diminishing gradient signal after the initial
        training epochs (Bengio et al., 2009) [VERIFY EXACT QUOTE
        BEFORE SUBMISSION].

    The 60-second margin was chosen to capture the temporal extent of
    peri-ictal phenomena observed in rodent models of epilepsy, where
    pre-ictal electrographic changes and post-ictal depression typically
    span tens of seconds (Luttjohann et al., 2009) [VERIFY EXACT QUOTE
    BEFORE SUBMISSION].

    The resulting balanced training manifest was generated once offline
    and reused without modification across all hyperparameter tuning and
    final model training runs. The binary cross-entropy loss pos_weight
    remained at 1.0, with the offline sampling serving as the sole
    imbalance correction mechanism.

9.3 Pre-ictal window sampling (alternative approach for comparison)

    To enable a head-to-head comparison between the seizure-detection
    formulation above (peri-ictal manifest) and a seizure-prediction
    formulation of the same binary task, a second training manifest
    was constructed using a fixed pre-ictal window strategy. For each
    annotated seizure with onset T (seconds from recording start),
    only non-ictal segments whose boundaries satisfied
    [T - 120 s, T - 60 s] under full containment
    (seg_start >= T - 120 AND seg_end <= T - 60) were retained:

    (a) A 60-second pre-ictal window of interest (the "seizure
        occurrence period", SOP) was placed 60 seconds before seizure
        onset, the latter 60-second interval acting as a "seizure
        prediction horizon" (SPH). The SPH was reserved specifically
        to exclude the immediately preceding peri-ictal transients
        and pre-ictal spikes that blur the classifier's decision
        boundary with ictal activity (Maiwald et al., 2004; Litt
        et al., 2001) [VERIFY EXACT QUOTE BEFORE SUBMISSION].

    (b) All other non-ictal segments (post-ictal, inter-ictal >120 s
        from any seizure, and the 60 s immediately preceding seizure
        onset) were DROPPED. No random subsample of distant background
        was drawn, in contrast to the peri-ictal manifest above.

    (c) Seizures whose onset fell less than 120 seconds into a
        recording have no usable pre-ictal window and are skipped for
        non-ictal selection; their ictal segments remain in the
        manifest.

    This design follows the "SPH + SOP" framework of Maiwald et al.
    (2004) [VERIFY EXACT QUOTE BEFORE SUBMISSION], in which SPH denotes
    the gap separating predicted-state evidence from ictal onset and
    SOP denotes the interval within which the predicted event must
    occur. The present configuration (SPH = SOP = 60 s) lies within
    the range of pre-ictal intervals where distinctive EEG dynamics
    have been reported in intracranial and scalp recordings
    (Mormann et al., 2007; Kuhlmann et al., 2018) [VERIFY EXACT QUOTE
    BEFORE SUBMISSION].

    Justification of the class imbalance treatment. Under full
    containment with 5-second windows and 2.5-second steps, the
    per-seizure ceiling on retained non-ictal segments is
    approximately (PREICTAL_WIDTH_SEC - 5) / 2.5 + 1 = 23. Across the
    2,881 annotated seizures of the training cohort the non-ictal
    count is bounded above by ~23 x 2,881 ~= 66,000 segments, a count
    of the same order as the 65,510 retained ictal segments. The
    resulting ictal-to-non-ictal ratio is therefore approximately 1:1
    (or ictal-majority), so NO additional class-reweighting is
    required: pos_weight = 1.0 is retained in BCEWithLogitsLoss,
    identical to the peri-ictal configuration. This selection-rule-
    driven balancing is consistent with the binary pre-ictal vs ictal
    (or pre-ictal vs inter-ictal) classification pipelines surveyed
    in Kuhlmann et al. (2018) and Rasheed et al. (2020) [VERIFY EXACT
    QUOTE BEFORE SUBMISSION], which rely on fixed-window selection
    rules rather than loss reweighting to achieve class balance.

    The two manifests (peri-ictal and pre-ictal) share the SAME
    data_splits.json input, the SAME exclusion criteria (m254
    removal, amplitude filter, NaN/Inf filter), the SAME validation
    pass-through, the SAME random seed (42), and the SAME .npy file
    universe on disk. Only the non-ictal selection rule differs, so
    any downstream differences in model performance between the two
    configurations are attributable to the selection rule alone. The
    pre-ictal manifest is generated by create_T_120_splits.py and
    written to data_splits_T_120_sampled.json.

10. DATA PROCESSING PIPELINE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The end-to-end data processing pipeline proceeds through four stages:

Stage 1: Signal preprocessing and segmentation
    Raw EDF recordings from 102 subjects are preprocessed (notch
    filtering, baseline correction, robust z-score normalisation) and
    segmented into 5-second windows with 50% overlap. Each segment is
    labelled as ictal or non-ictal and saved as an individual .npy file.
    Preprocessing is distributed across five parallel batch jobs.

Stage 2: Partition manifest generation
    All five training data directories, the validation directory, and
    (when available) the test directory are scanned to produce a single
    JSON manifest (data_splits.json) containing absolute file paths and
    labels for all 33 million segments. A subject-level leakage check
    is executed to verify partition integrity.

Stage 3: Training manifest construction
    The training partition of data_splits.json is processed by one of
    two offline scripts, each producing a manifest with the same JSON
    schema:

    (a) create_balanced_splits.py -> data_splits_nonictal_sampled.json
        (peri-ictal manifest; seizure-detection formulation). Excludes
        subject m254, removes amplitude-anomalous segments, classifies
        non-ictal segments as near-seizure (+/-60 s) or far, and
        applies proximity-aware downsampling with a 5% far-sample
        subsample of the near-seizure count (Section 9.2).

    (b) create_T_120_splits.py -> data_splits_T_120_sampled.json
        (pre-ictal manifest; seizure-prediction formulation). Applies
        the same exclusions, then retains only non-ictal segments
        fully contained in [T-120 s, T-60 s] for each annotated
        seizure onset T. Drops post-ictal, inter-ictal, and immediate
        pre-ictal (<60 s) segments (Section 9.3).

    Either manifest serves as the training source for subsequent
    experiments; both are supported by downstream scripts via a
    manifest-path argument. The validation partition is passed through
    unmodified in both cases.

Stage 4: Model development
    Hyperparameter tuning (4 scripts) and final model training
    (4 scripts) load whichever manifest (peri-ictal or pre-ictal) is
    under evaluation. No further downsampling, filtering, or data
    manipulation is performed at training time.

12. REPRODUCIBILITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The following measures were implemented to ensure full reproducibility
of all experimental results:

(i)   Deterministic seeding. A fixed random seed (42) was set for
      Python's random module, NumPy, and all PyTorch random number
      generators (CPU and CUDA) at the start of every script.

(ii)  Deterministic preprocessing. All preprocessing, segmentation,
      and labelling operations are fully deterministic given identical
      input EDF and annotation files.

(iii) Partition integrity. The subject-level leakage check is enforced
      programmatically at manifest generation time, ensuring no subject
      appears in more than one partition.

(iv)  Manifest-based sampling. All downsampling decisions are recorded
      in JSON manifests generated once and reused across experiments.
      The original .npy segment files are never modified, deleted, or
      moved by any downstream script, preserving the complete dataset
      for alternative analyses (e.g., self-supervised learning).

(v)   Software environment. Experiments were conducted using PyTorch
      2.5.1 with CUDA 11.8 on NVIDIA V100, A100, and L40S GPUs
      managed by the Slurm workload scheduler.

ADDENDUM A: GLOSSARY OF DEEP LEARNING CONCEPTS
================================================================================

This addendum provides intuitive explanations of the deep learning
concepts used throughout this report, aimed at readers who may be
new to the field.

A.1 Uniform vs weighted averaging (pooling vs attention)

    After a convolutional network processes a 5-second EEG segment,
    it produces a feature vector h_t at every time step t = 1, ..., T
    (where T = 2,500 at 500 Hz). Think of each h_t as a summary
    description of "what the EEG looks like" at that moment in time.

    The problem: the classifier expects a single fixed-size input,
    but we have 2,500 of these descriptions. We need to collapse
    them into one.

    GLOBAL AVERAGE POOLING (uniform averaging):
        z = (h_1 + h_2 + ... + h_T) / T

        Every time step contributes equally. Time step 1 counts as
        much as time step 2,500. This is like reading a 5-second
        EEG epoch and summarising it by averaging every moment
        equally -- no moment is considered more important than
        another.

        Used in: M1 (TCN) and M3 (Multi-Scale TCN).

    TEMPORAL ATTENTION (weighted averaging):
        c = alpha_1*h_1 + alpha_2*h_2 + ... + alpha_T*h_T

        Each time step gets its own learned weight alpha_t. The
        model learns from the data which moments are most
        informative for detecting seizures. If a seizure starts
        at time step 1,500, the model might assign alpha_1500 = 0.3
        (high attention) while giving alpha_100 = 0.001 (almost no
        attention) to a quiet background period.

        The weights alpha_t are non-negative and sum to 1 (enforced
        by the softmax function), so this is a weighted average --
        just not a uniform one.

        Used in: M2 (TCN + Attention) and M4 (Multi-Scale TCN +
        Attention).

    WHY THIS MATTERS: if seizure-discriminative EEG patterns are
    concentrated in a specific part of the 5-second window (e.g.,
    the onset), uniform averaging dilutes that signal with 4 seconds
    of uninformative background. Attention can amplify the onset and
    suppress the background, producing a cleaner signal for the
    classifier. The ablation study tests whether this learnable
    focusing actually improves detection accuracy.

A.2 Logit, sigmoid, and probability

    A logit is the raw, unbounded output of the classification head
    -- a single number that can range from negative infinity to
    positive infinity. It represents the model's "unnormalised
    confidence" that the input is a seizure.

        logit = -3.0  ->  model is fairly confident: NOT a seizure
        logit =  0.0  ->  model is uncertain (50/50)
        logit = +5.0  ->  model is very confident: IS a seizure

    The sigmoid function squashes the logit into the range [0, 1],
    making it interpretable as a probability:

        p = 1 / (1 + exp(-logit))

        logit = -3.0  ->  p = 0.047  (4.7% chance of seizure)
        logit =  0.0  ->  p = 0.500  (50% chance)
        logit = +5.0  ->  p = 0.993  (99.3% chance)

    A threshold (default 0.5) is then applied: if p >= 0.5, the
    segment is classified as ictal; otherwise, non-ictal.

A.3 Loss function

    The loss function measures how wrong the model's prediction is
    for a single training example, producing a number that the
    optimiser tries to minimise.

    Binary cross-entropy (BCE) is the standard loss for two-class
    problems. Intuitively:

        If the true label is SEIZURE (y=1) and the model predicts
        p=0.95, the loss is small (the model is right).

        If the true label is SEIZURE (y=1) and the model predicts
        p=0.05, the loss is large (the model is very wrong).

    The formula:
        L = -[y * log(p) + (1-y) * log(1-p)]

    BCEWithLogitsLoss combines the sigmoid and BCE into one step
    for numerical stability -- it avoids computing log(sigmoid(x))
    separately, which can produce infinity or NaN for very large
    or very small logits.

A.4 Gradient, backpropagation, and gradient clipping

    GRADIENT: for each trainable parameter (e.g., a convolution
    filter weight), the gradient tells the optimiser "if I increase
    this weight slightly, how much does the loss change?" A positive
    gradient means increasing the weight increases the loss (bad),
    so the optimiser decreases it.

    BACKPROPAGATION: the algorithm that computes gradients for all
    parameters simultaneously by propagating the loss backward
    through the network, layer by layer, using the chain rule of
    calculus.

    GRADIENT CLIPPING: in deep networks, gradients can occasionally
    become very large ("exploding gradients"), causing the optimiser
    to take a catastrophically large step that destabilises training.
    Gradient clipping limits the total gradient magnitude to a
    maximum norm (1.0 in this study): if the combined gradient
    across all parameters exceeds 1.0, every gradient is scaled
    down proportionally so the total norm equals exactly 1.0.

A.5 Learning rate, weight decay, and AdamW

    LEARNING RATE (lr): controls how large a step the optimiser
    takes at each update. Too large and training becomes unstable
    (the model overshoots good solutions); too small and training
    is extremely slow or gets stuck.

    WEIGHT DECAY (wd): a regularisation technique that penalises
    large parameter values by adding a small fraction of each
    weight's magnitude to the loss. This discourages overly
    complex models and reduces overfitting. A weight decay of 1e-4
    means "at each step, shrink every weight by 0.01%."

    AdamW: an optimiser that adapts the learning rate individually
    for each parameter based on the history of its gradients. The
    "W" indicates decoupled weight decay (Loshchilov & Hutter,
    2019), which applies weight decay directly to the parameters
    rather than through the gradient, preventing the regularisation
    strength from diminishing as the learning rate decays.

    COSINE ANNEALING: the learning rate starts at its initial value
    and smoothly decreases following a half-cosine curve over the
    training budget (20 epochs for tuning, 100 for final training),
    reaching a minimum of 1% of the initial value. This allows
    aggressive exploration early in training and fine-grained
    convergence later.

A.6 Batch size and gradient noise

    Training data is processed in batches (groups of segments).
    The gradient computed from one batch is an approximation of the
    true gradient over the entire dataset.

    SMALL BATCHES (e.g., 16): noisier gradient estimates, but each
    step is cheaper and the noise can help the optimiser escape
    shallow local minima.

    LARGE BATCHES (e.g., 64): smoother gradient estimates, but each
    step is more expensive and the optimiser may converge to sharper
    (potentially less generalisable) minima.

    The batch size also affects GPU memory usage: larger batches
    require more VRAM. Powers of 2 (16, 32, 64) are preferred
    because GPU hardware is optimised for these sizes.

A.7 Dropout and regularisation

    REGULARISATION: any technique that prevents a model from
    memorising the training data (overfitting) and encourages it
    to learn general patterns that transfer to unseen data.

    DROPOUT: during training, a random subset of neurons (or entire
    channels) is temporarily set to zero at each forward pass. This
    forces the network to learn redundant representations that do
    not rely on any single neuron, improving generalisation.

    SPATIAL DROPOUT (Dropout1d): instead of dropping individual
    values, entire convolutional feature-map channels are dropped
    across the full time dimension. Because adjacent time steps
    within a single feature map share overlapping receptive fields
    (and are therefore highly correlated), element-wise dropout on
    a single cell is easily recovered from its temporal neighbours;
    spatial dropout avoids this by removing the entire feature map,
    forcing the remaining feature maps to carry the signal.

    Terminology note: "channel" here means a learned convolutional
    feature map, not an EEG recording electrode. The recordings in
    this study are single-electrode, so the input tensor has shape
    (batch, 1, time) and the num_filters feature-map dimension is
    introduced only after the first convolution. Dropping a feature
    map does not drop a physical electrode.

    The dropout rate (e.g., 0.30) specifies the probability that
    each feature map is dropped: at 0.30, roughly 30% of the
    num_filters feature maps are zeroed out in each training batch.
    Dropout is disabled during evaluation (model.eval()), so all
    feature maps contribute to the final prediction.

A.8 Early stopping and patience

    EARLY STOPPING: a technique to prevent overfitting by monitoring
    a validation metric (macro F1-score in this study) after each
    training epoch. Training is halted when the metric stops
    improving.

    PATIENCE: the number of consecutive epochs without improvement
    before training is stopped. A patience of 5 means: "if the
    validation F1 has not exceeded its best value for 5 epochs in
    a row, stop training and keep the best model."

    This prevents the model from training past its peak performance
    into the overfitting regime, where training loss continues to
    decrease but validation performance degrades.

A.9 Mixed precision (AMP)

    Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) uses 16-bit floating-point
    numbers (FP16) instead of 32-bit (FP32) for the forward and
    backward passes through the network. This approximately halves
    memory usage and can double throughput on GPUs with dedicated
    FP16 hardware (Tensor Cores on V100, A100, L40S).

    The GradScaler dynamically adjusts a scaling factor to prevent
    the smaller FP16 number range from causing gradient underflow
    (gradients too small to represent, rounded to zero). Weight
    updates are always performed in FP32 to maintain precision
    where it matters most.

A.10 Receptive field

    The receptive field of a convolutional network is the span of
    input samples that can influence a single output value. In a
    TCN with dilated convolutions, the receptive field grows
    exponentially with depth:

        Layer 1 (dilation 1):  each output sees 7 input samples (k=7)
        Layer 2 (dilation 2):  each output sees 19 input samples
        Layer 3 (dilation 4):  each output sees 43 input samples
        ...
        Layer 6 (dilation 32): each output sees 757 input samples

    A receptive field of 757 samples at 500 Hz means the network
    can "see" 1.514 seconds of EEG history when making a prediction
    at any given time step. If the receptive field is too small
    (e.g., 125 samples = 0.25 s), the network cannot capture
    seizure patterns that evolve over longer timescales.
